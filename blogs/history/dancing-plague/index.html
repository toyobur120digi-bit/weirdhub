The Dancing Plague of 1518: When an Entire Town Couldn't Stop Dancing

It began with a single woman dancing in the street. Within days, hundreds had joined her. Some danced for weeks. Dozens died from exhaustion, heart attacks, and strokes. This is one of the strangest events in recorded history.

How It Started

In July 1518, a woman named Frau Troffea stepped into the streets of Strasbourg (then part of the Holy Roman Empire) and began dancing. She showed no signs of joy — her movements were frantic, almost desperate. She danced through the night and into the next day without stopping.

Within a week, around 34 others had joined her. By the end of the month, the number had grown to over 400. The dancers showed clear signs of distress. Some screamed in pain. Others begged for help but could not stop their bodies from moving.

Local physicians were baffled. They eventually concluded that the only “cure” was more dancing — so they built a stage and hired musicians to keep the afflicted moving until they collapsed from exhaustion.

Why Did This Happen?

Modern historians and psychologists believe the Dancing Plague was a classic case of mass psychogenic illness (also called mass hysteria). In times of extreme stress — famine, disease, religious upheaval — groups of people can unconsciously manifest physical symptoms with no organic cause.

Strasbourg in 1518 was suffering from severe food shortages, the aftermath of the Black Death, and intense religious tension between Catholics and emerging Protestant movements. The psychological pressure was immense.

The dancing itself may have been a form of religious expression that spiraled out of control, or a physical manifestation of overwhelming anxiety and despair that spread through social contagion.

The Aftermath and Legacy

The plague eventually burned itself out after several months. Many of the dancers died. Those who survived were left physically broken and deeply traumatized.

While similar dancing manias had occurred in Europe before (notably in the 14th century), the 1518 outbreak remains the best documented and most extreme example. It stands as a powerful reminder of how fragile the human mind and body can be when pushed to the brink by collective suffering.

Today, the Dancing Plague of 1518 is studied by psychologists, historians, and sociologists as one of the most dramatic examples of how stress and belief can literally move mountains — or in this case, entire towns full of people.
